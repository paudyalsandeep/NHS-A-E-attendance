1. Operations/Analytics: Workforce Scheduling & Capacity Planner
Idea: Build an analytics + optimisation project that helps a service business (e.g., call centre, hospital ward, delivery fleet) decide how many staff/vehicles to schedule per hour/day based on historical demand.

Stack focus: SQL + Python + Power BI.

What you do:

Source data: Use an open dataset with timestamps and volume (e.g., 999 calls, taxi trips, bike sharing, hospital admissions, or your own synthetic dataset).

SQL:

Design a small warehouse-style schema: fact table for events, dimension tables for date/time, location, staff.

Write complex queries: window functions to get rolling demand, peak/off‑peak patterns, service levels, etc.

Python:

Time-series modelling (Prophet, SARIMA, or gradient boosting) to forecast demand per time slot.

Simple optimisation: given forecasted demand and constraints (max hours per person, min coverage), build a linear/integer programming model (PuLP or OR‑Tools) to generate an optimal staffing schedule.

Power BI:

“Operations cockpit” with:

Demand forecast vs actual.

Recommended staffing vs current staffing.

Heatmap of under/over‑staffing by hour/day.

What it proves on your CV:

Strong SQL for analytics and modelling data.

Applied time‑series forecasting and basic optimisation (a bit rarer than classification/regression).

Ability to turn models into an actionable dashboard and narrative (very appealing for analyst/DS roles).

How to title it:

“Workforce Capacity Planning: Forecast‑Driven Scheduling for a Call Centre”

2. Product/Data Science: Pricing & Revenue Optimisation for a Marketplace
Idea: Model demand sensitivity to price and build a small pricing simulator for a marketplace or e‑commerce setting.

Stack focus: Python (ML), Excel/Power BI, SQL.

What you do:

Data:

Use a public e‑commerce/retail dataset or simulate transaction data with price, units sold, discount, competitor price, category, seasonality.

SQL:

Build KPIs: revenue, margin, conversion rate by product, segment, time, and promo.

Python:

Feature engineering: price buckets, lagged sales, competitor price ratios, promo flags, holiday flags.

Model:

Regression or gradient boosting to predict demand as a function of price and other factors.

Estimate price elasticity for each product or segment.

Scenario engine:

Given a price change, simulate impact on volume, revenue, and profit.

Excel/Power BI:

Simple “what‑if” pricing dashboard:

Slicers to change price or discount band.

Charts showing projected revenue and profit.

What it proves on your CV:

You understand commercial metrics (revenue, margin, elasticity) not just model accuracy.

You can connect ML predictions to business decisions (price changes and their impact).

Good fit for product analytics, revenue analytics, and DS roles in retail, travel, or marketplaces.

How to title it:

“Dynamic Pricing and Revenue Impact Simulator for an Online Marketplace”

3. Customer/Data Analytics: User Segmentation & Churn Storytelling
Idea: Build a segmentation and churn‑risk story for a subscription product (SaaS, telecom, streaming), but focus on storytelling and stakeholder communication, not just the model.

Stack focus: SQL + Python + Power BI/Excel (presentation).

What you do:

Data:

Use a subscription or telco dataset, or Kaggle‑style customer data, but don’t stop at a vanilla churn model.

SQL:

Derive behavioural features: usage frequency, recency, average spend, complaint rate, support contacts, plan changes.

Python:

Unsupervised learning:

Clustering (K‑Means, GMM) to segment customers into behavior‑based groups.

Supervised learning:

Churn prediction model (logistic regression, XGBoost).

Interpretability:

SHAP or feature importance plots for churn drivers by segment.

Business story:

For 2–3 key segments, propose specific retention actions (discounts, feature education, support outreach) and estimate impact (e.g., if we target top 20% risk, we can reduce churn by X% and save £Y per year, based on assumptions).

Power BI / slides:

Segment profiles (size, value, churn rate).

“Churn drivers” visual and action plan summary.

What it proves on your CV:

Segmentation and predictive modelling that look like real product/marketing analytics.

You can go from model output to prioritised actions and an executive‑style deck, which hiring managers care about a lot.

Strong candidate for data analyst, customer insights, or product data roles.

How to title it:

“Customer Segmentation and Churn‑Reduction Strategy for a Subscription Business”

4. Data Engineering / MLOps: End‑to‑End Data Pipeline & Monitoring
Idea: Show that you can build a small but realistic end‑to‑end pipeline: ingestion → cleaning → modelling → dashboarding → monitoring.

Stack focus: Python + SQL + basic orchestration + Power BI.

What you do:

Choose a setting:

For example: daily air quality index per city, daily hotel demand, or daily bike‑sharing usage.

Pipeline:

Ingestion:

Pull data from an API or CSV directory with a scheduled script (e.g., cron, Windows task scheduler).

Storage:

Load into a SQL database (or SQLite/Postgres) with a clean schema.

Modelling:

Daily Python job that trains/updates a simple forecasting or anomaly detection model and writes predictions back to the DB.

Monitoring:

Compute model drift or performance metrics over time (MAPE, RMSE, etc.).

Power BI:

“ML Monitoring” dashboard showing:

Recent predictions vs actuals.

Model error trends.

Flags when error exceeds a threshold.

What it proves on your CV:

You understand the lifecycle beyond Jupyter notebooks: ingestion, storage, scheduled jobs, monitoring.

You’re comfortable with basic data engineering, which is extremely valuable even for DS/DA roles.

Signals that you can contribute to production‑oriented work, not just offline analysis.

How to title it:

“End‑to‑End Data Pipeline with Automated Forecasting and Model Monitoring”
